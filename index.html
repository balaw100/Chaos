Bifurcation · Period Doubling
x_next = r · x · (1 − x)
LOGISTIC MAP EXPLORER

One equation. One slider. Watch a population model tip from stability into chaos — and find that chaos isn't random at all.

Launch →
Why It Matters

In 1976, biologist Robert May showed that this simple population equation could produce behavior so complex it looked random. It wasn't — it was deterministic chaos. The same input always gives the same output, but tiny changes in r make the long-term behavior impossible to predict. This discovery shook science: complexity doesn't require complex rules.

How to Use It

The r slider is everything. Drag it right and watch the single stable point split into two, then four — period doubling. Past r≈3.57, order collapses into chaos. The bifurcation diagram shows this across all values of r at once. The orbit diagram below is a slice in time — it shows how the population evolves step by step at your chosen r. Double-click the bifurcation diagram to jump r to any point. Zoom into the chaotic region to find windows of order hiding inside.

DOUBLE PENDULUM

Two pendulums, identical to 1/100,000th of a degree. Within seconds they diverge completely. This is the butterfly effect — not a metaphor, a demonstration.

Launch →
Why It Matters

The double pendulum is the simplest physical system that exhibits true chaos. No matter how precisely you set the initial conditions, tiny unmeasurable differences grow exponentially until the two paths bear no resemblance to each other. This is why long-range weather forecasting has hard limits — not because our computers are too slow, but because the physics itself amplifies uncertainty.

How to Use It

Drag either mass to position it, then release. Watch the divergence percentage climb in the right panel. Start high for maximum chaos, low for more orderly motion. Try the Show Phase Space button — instead of plotting position, it graphs angle against angular velocity, revealing the underlying structure of the motion in a way the physical view can't show.

STRANGE ATTRACTORS

A path that wanders forever through the same region of space, never repeating, never escaping. This is a strange attractor — chaos with a shape.

Launch →
Why It Matters

In 1963, Edward Lorenz discovered that a simple three-equation weather model produced a trajectory that never repeated yet stayed bounded forever. The shape it traced — the Lorenz butterfly — became the icon of chaos theory. What makes it truly remarkable: the path never crosses itself. Ever. Infinite complexity folded into a finite region, with zero repetition. Strange attractors appear throughout nature — in heartbeats, brain waves, and chemical reactions — showing that chaotic systems aren't directionless. They have structure, just not the kind we're used to.

How to Use It

Select an attractor at the top and drag to rotate in 3D. Watch the X·Y·Z time series below — those irregular oscillations are the chaos signature. Notice that the values never blow up to infinity and never settle to zero: the system is permanently trapped in bounded disorder. Adjust sigma and rho to watch the attractor change shape — at certain values it collapses to a simple orbit, then explodes back into chaos.

MANDELBROT & JULIA SETS

Zoom into the boundary and it never simplifies. New detail appears forever. The Mandelbrot set is the most complex object in mathematics — generated by one equation, iterated over and over.

Launch →
Why It Matters

Benoit Mandelbrot discovered in 1980 that iterating one simple complex equation produces infinite self-similar structure at every scale. The Mandelbrot set is essentially an index of all possible Julia sets — every point on its boundary corresponds to a unique fractal in the right panel. This was the founding insight of fractal geometry: a new mathematical language for describing the roughness and complexity found throughout nature.

How to Use It

Scroll to zoom into the Mandelbrot boundary on the left — you can go infinitely deep and new structure always appears. Hover your cursor over any point and watch the corresponding Julia set appear live on the right. Points inside the black region produce connected Julia sets; points outside produce scattered dust. The most intricate Julia sets live right on the boundary between the two.

SIERPINSKI TRIANGLE

Pick a random vertex. Move halfway toward it. Leave a dot. That's the whole rule. Watch what emerges.

Launch →
Why It Matters

The Sierpinski Triangle is one of the most striking examples of order emerging from randomness. There is no blueprint, no artist, no planning — just one rule applied over and over. Yet a perfect fractal appears, the same at every scale. It was first described by mathematician Wacław Sierpiński in 1915, but the chaos game method of generating it — purely through random choices — wasn't discovered until 1988. The pattern never repeats exactly, yet the structure is always identical.

How to Use It

Press Begin and watch the rule being taught step by step — each dot placed halfway between the last and a randomly chosen vertex. Then watch thousands of dots accumulate. As it zooms in, the same triangle appears inside itself, then again inside that — the fractal goes as deep as the dots allow. The tool demonstrates that complexity doesn't require complex rules. Sometimes the simplest instruction, repeated blindly, reveals something extraordinary.
